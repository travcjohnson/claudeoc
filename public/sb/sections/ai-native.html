Five rungs · each one roughly 10× the last

RungWhat it looks likeWhat blocks youTo climb one rung
0 0 agents
Gated
“We’re still evaluating it.” Your team uses AI on their phones anyway.
Legacy security and approval process. No technical voice in the decision.
Get an exec to own it and escalate the blockers. Give security a safe, approved way to say yes.
1 ~1 agent
Assisted
One person, one chat, one task. You watch every word it writes.
Your own attention. You feel you must read everything.
Build a check the machine runs itself — and trust it. Then run more than one agent at a time.
2 ~10 agents
Parallel
One person runs 5–10 agents at once. The machine checks the work before you see it.
Review. You’re now reading six streams of output.
Give it your context — docs, tickets, wikis. Turn repeat work into standing routines.
3 ~100 agents
Supervised autonomy
Nobody starts the work. It starts itself. Your people handle what breaks.
Trust in the loop, and your team’s decision speed.
Automate a whole domain end to end, not single tasks.
4 ~1,000+ agents
AI-native
You state an outcome. A fleet runs it. You monitor by exception.
Finding the work worth doing, and the guardrails for each kind.
You’re at the top. Now widen it.

Why the ladder keeps moving

The length of a job AI can finish
doubles about every 7 months.

Every 7 months, the job you thought was too big for AI becomes the job it finishes.

That is why rung 2 today looks like rung 3 next year. The ladder moves under you.

METR’s own range: 1–4 doublings per year. Above 16 hours their task suite stops being reliable.

Source: METR, “Measuring AI Ability to Complete Long Tasks” · metr.org/time-horizons

What actually gets you off rung one

01 You describe the work
↓
02 Agents do it — in parallel
↓
03 The machine checks its own work
↓
04 You review finished output

Step three is the whole ladder.

If the machine can check its own work in a way you trust, you can stop reading everything — and one person can run ten agents instead of one.

Engineering gets this almost free: tests, build, lint. Nobody else does. There’s no compiler for a contract and no test suite for a brand.

Building that check is the work.

Engineering

Support
You have to build the check
The queue that never shrinks Ticket backlog. A help center nobody has updated.
Its “tests pass” Does it follow policy and cite a real doc?
Rung 2 looks like One person rewrites the help center and clears triage.

Sales

The queue that never shrinks The long-tail list nobody has touched
Its “tests pass” Is every claim true to the account data?

Now do it for your department

Four questions. Answer them and you’ve climbed a rung.

1
What is your team’s “tests pass”?
The check that lets you stop reading everything.
2
What’s the queue that never shrinks?
That’s where you prove it first. Everyone has one.
3
What should start itself?
Which events should kick off work unasked. That’s rung 3.
4
What stays human, permanently?
The line that lets you say yes to all of the above.
