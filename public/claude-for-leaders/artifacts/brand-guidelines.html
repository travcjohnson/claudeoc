Brand Guidelines · v1.0

Harborline Advisors

A visual and verbal identity system for a coastal California wealth-advisory boutique. Calm, precise, and built to signal one thing above all: your capital is in steady hands.

Prepared by Claude Cowork Client Harborline Advisors (fictional) Date July 2026
01

Logo concept

The wordmark pairs a quiet serif with a horizon mark: three lines settling into stillness inside a circle — the harbor line at dusk. It reads as maritime without a single anchor or sailboat cliché.

Primary · on light
Reversed · on Harbor Navy

Construction

The mark is built from a horizon line and two receding swells inside a perfect circle. The brass wave is the only warm element — it carries through every application as the brand’s signature stroke.

Clearspace & minimum size

Keep clearspace equal to the circle’s radius on all sides. Never render the lockup below 120px wide on screen or 28mm in print; below that, use the circular mark alone.

Color rules

Two colorways only: Harbor Navy on light, Gull White reversed on navy. The brass wave never changes. No gradients, outlines, or drop shadows on the lockup.

04

Voice & tone

Harborline sounds like the most experienced person in the room — the one who doesn’t need to raise their voice. Three principles govern everything we write.

P1

Calm over clever

We never chase urgency, hype, or fear. Markets are loud; we are not. If a sentence would sound wrong read aloud in a quiet room, cut it.

“Volatility is the fee, not the fine.”

P2

Plain before polished

Jargon is a tax on the reader. Say “what you keep after taxes,” not “tax-alpha optimization.” Precision and plain English are the same discipline.

“Here is what we’d do, and here is why.”

P3

Candor builds trust

We name trade-offs, fees, and what we don’t know. A client who hears the hard part from us first never has to hear it from someone else.

“This plan costs more in year one. It should.”

05

Do & don’t

The fastest way to protect the brand is knowing where it breaks. These are the lines we hold.

✓ Do
  • Lead with navy, accent with brassOne brass moment per layout — a number, a rule, a single call to action.
  • Use the horizon mark alone at small sizesFavicons, avatars, and app icons get the circle mark, never the squeezed lockup.
  • Photograph the coast at off-hoursEarly light, empty piers, still water. People appear candid, never posed at a whiteboard.
  • Set numbers in JetBrains MonoReturns, fees, and dates in mono — it signals accuracy and keeps tables honest.
✕ Don’t
  • Don’t stretch, tilt, or re-color the lockupNo gradients, outlines, shadows, or “just this once” palette swaps.
  • Don’t use nautical clip artNo anchors, ropes, ship wheels, or lighthouses. The horizon mark carries the metaphor alone.
  • Don’t write in fear or hypeNo “don’t miss out,” no countdown timers, no exclamation points in client-facing copy.
  • Don’t set body copy in Fraunces or SeafoamThe serif is for headlines; the tint is for surfaces. Body text is Inter on navy or ink.
